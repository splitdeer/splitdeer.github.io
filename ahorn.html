In Arthur Éloffe's "Histoire naturelle des cornes" (Natural History of Horns), we find clues as to the relationship between Réaumur's work with insects and the stag's head. I stumbled upon this connective tissue as I attempted to translate the French phrase "la chute du bois" from Réaumur's description of the Plate figures. Literally the phrase means "the fall of wood"; "auxquels les Chasseurs attribuent la chute du bois" translated literally is "to which the Hunters attribute the fall of the wood."

I was having trouble parsing out the context of this phrase, knowing it had to do with the deer and the worms and yet unable to find any resources that might help me make sense of "the fall of the wood" in this context. That is, until I randomly stumbled upon a section of Éloffe's odd & illuminating volume, discussing the horns of "le cerf ordinaire" (the common deer), in the digital archives of the Bibliothèque nationale de France.

The section opens by citing the work of Réaumur's young rival, the naturalist Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon:

"Buffon explique ainsi la formation des cornes de cerf : « La corne ou le bois de cerf, ne s’étend que par Tune de ses extrémités; l’autre lui sert de point d’appui. Il est d’abord tendre comme l’herbe et se durcit ensuite comme le bois : la peau qui s’étend et qui croît avec lui est son écorce, et il s’en dépouille lorsqu’il a pris son entier accroissement; tant qu’il croît, l’extrémité supérieure demeure toujours molle. 11 se divise aussi en plusieurs rameaux; le merrain est l’arbre, les andouillers en sont les branches. En un mot, tout est semblable, tout est conforme dans le développement et dans l’accroissement de l’un et de l’autre, et dès lors les molécules organiques retiennent encore l’empreinte du végétal, parce qu’elles s’arrangent de la même façon que dans les végétaux. » "

Buffon thus explains the formation of the horns of deer: "The horn or the antler, extends only by one of its extremities; the other end is a point of support. At first the antler is tender like grass and then hardens like wood: the skin that extends and grows along with it is its bark, and it truly is a skin when it has fully grown; as long as it grows, the upper ends stay soft. The antler also divides into several branches; the stave is the tree, the antlers are its branches. In a word, all forms in Nature are similar... the organic molecules still retain the imprint of the plant, because they are arranged in the same way as in plants."

"Les causes de la chute si singulière du bois des cerfs sont encore inconnues.
Au printemps, lorsque se montre la végétation, le bois tombe;
peut-on l’attribuer à une exfoliation naturelle? Peut-on penser que le bois, après le sixième mois,
découvert de ses téguments,sans aucune communication avec les vaisseaux nourriciers, meure enfin sur la tête de l’animal;
se détache peu à peu avec le secours du contact de l’air et celui de son poids?
Non, sans doute, puisque le bois persiste toute la vie sur la tête du cerf qui a été soumis à la castration dans le temps où il était muni de son bois';
fait incompréhensible, mais qui démontre qu’il y a des rapports entre le bois de cerf et les organes de la génération.
Pour découvrir à quoi tient cette chute du bois, il faudrait observer la manière dont il se détache presque naturellement.
C’est un fait qui dépend de causes physiques que l’on découvrira sans doute un jour."

The causes of the unique phenomenon of deer shedding their antlers are still unknown. In the spring, when the rough skin of the antler becomes visible, it falls off; is this a result of natural exfoliation? Are we to think that the antlers, after six months, outgrow their protective outer layer (integument) and, unable to communicate with the nourishing vessels [of the the vascular “velvet” skin which provide oxygen and blood to the growing bone] finally die on the head of the animal and gradually fall off with the help of exposure to the elements and the antler’s own weight? This is doubtful, since the antlers on the head of a castrated stag remain present for the entirety of its life; it's an incomprehensible phenomenon, yet it demonstrates that there are links between deer antlers and the reproductive organs. To discover what causes the antlers to fall off, one would have to observe the way in which they detach in the wild. This fact depends on physical causes that we will no doubt one day discover.

Aristotle, too, admits an uncertain understanding of just how a stag goes about shedding its antlers:

"He sheds his horns in difficult and scarcely accessible places, from whence arises the proverb, 'where the stag sheds its horns,' for they are afraid of being seen, as if they had lost their means of defense. It is said that the left horn never has been seen, for he conceals it as if it had some medicinal power."

Éloffe continues, revealing the link between the work of Buffon, Réaumur, the botfly larvae, and the mystery of "the fall of the wood":

"On croyait autrefois que la chute du bois des cerfs était due à des larves de mouches qui rongeaient le merrain ou perche à l’endroit où il sort de la meule. Réaumur (tome V de ses Mémoires) combat cette erreur, et constate l’absence de ces larves à la base des bois de cerfs tués au mois de mars, au moment de la chute des bois, et qui lui étaient envoyés pour cette étude par le prince de Conti."

It was once believed that deer antlers fell off as a result of fly larvae gnawing at the base [the pedicle] of the stave or branch. Réaumur (in volume V of his Memoirs) combats this misunderstanding, and observes that such larvae were absent at the base of the antlers of a deer that was killed in March, when its antlers fell off; [the same deer that was] sent to him by the Prince de Conti for this study.
