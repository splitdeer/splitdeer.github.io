In every crowd, there is the one
with horns, casually moving through
the bodies as if this is the living

room of a creature with horns,
a long cloak and the song of tongues
on the lips of the body. To see

the horns, one’s heart rate must
reach one hundred and seventy
five beats per minute, at a rate

faster than the blink of an eye,
for the body with horns lives
in the space between the blink

and somewhere in the white space
between sight and sightlessness

Arthur Éloffe’s “Histoire naturelle des cornes : définition, composition, forme des cornes..” (Natural history of horns: definition, composition, shape of horns..) is one of the most intriguing and disturbing texts I have stumbled across in recent memory.

The images above have been taken directly from the text itself, where they are used as reference images for Éloffe’s comprehensive study. I undertook the exhaustive project of translating some of the chapters, with the hopes of discovering just who or what these images could be referring to.

Within this work of “natural history,” my answers were to be found in the chapter titled “Cornes Contre Nature” (Unnatural Horns):

La présence de cornes insolites est assez commune sur les animaux, les moutons en offrent plusieurs exemples, on en a vu sur des bœufs, des chiens, des lièvres, et M. Marche possède, dans son intéressant musée, une tête de renard portant une corne (fig. 91).

The presence of unusual horns is quite common on animals, sheep offer several examples, we have seen them on oxen, dogs, hares, and M. Marche has in his interesting museum, a fox's head with a horn (fig. 91).

Il y a eu dans les écuries du Roi d’Espagne un cheval cornu et dans celles de Frédéric III, Roi de Danemarck, un cheval qui présentait le même phénomène; il avait deux cornes qui naissaient de la partie inférieure et externe de chaque oreille; elles étaient recourbées, pointues, assez semblables à un ergot de coq et longues de 67 centimètres… Une monstruosité plus étonnante est celle de la croissance de véritables cornes sur le corps humain.

There was a horned horse in the stables of the King of Spain and in those of Frederick III, King of Danemarck, a horse that presented the same phenomenon; he had two horns that sprung from the lower and outer part of each ear; they were curved, pointed, quite similar to a rooster's spur and 67 centimeters long… A more astonishing monstrosity is that of the growth of real horns on the human body.

Thomas Bartliolinus, dans l’ouvrage déjà cité qui traite des différentes espèces d’animaux à une seule corne, et dans un premier chapitre consacré aux faits de ce genre qui ont été observés cliez l’homme, raconte notamment ceux qui suivent :

Thomas Bartliolinus, in a work… which deals with different species of animals with only one horn, in an early chapter devoted to types of this phenomena that have been observed in man, recounts the following:

En 1599, Auguste Thuanus a écrit... que l’on remarqua dans tout le pays un homme qui parcourut différentes contrées en faisant voir, moyennant une certaine somme, une corne qui lui était poussée au milieu du front.

In 1599, Auguste Thuanus wrote... that many people throughout the country had encountered a man who traveled around various lands showing, for a certain sum, a single horn protruding from the middle of his forehead.

Bartholinus lui-même vit en 1646 une pauvre vieille femme de Purmeront en Hollande, nommée Marguerite Mainers, qui avait sur le côté droit de la tête, au-dessus de l’os temporal, une corne longue de 12 doigts ou d’environ 16 centimètres. Cette corne, qui avait beaucoup de ressemblance avec celle d’une chèvre, après avoir décrit un arc de cercle autour de l’os temporal, se courbait de nouveau vers l’extrémité de la tête. Elle avait une couleur pourpre à la racine et laissait écouler du sang dès qu’on venait à la toucher un peu fortement.

Bartholinus himself saw, in 1646, a poor old woman from Purmeront in Holland, named Marguerite Mainers, who had on the right side of her head, above the temporal bone, a horn approximately 12 fingers or 16 centimeters long. This horn, which had much resemblance to that of a goat, after drawing a circular arc around the temporal bone, curved again towards the back of the head. It had a purple color at the root and would begin to bleed if it were touched too forcefully.

The entire chapter is dedicated to accounts such as these, detailing encounters with horned humans. Most of the stories are rather pitiful.

On trouve dans le journal de l'Estoile pour le règne de Henri IV le passage suivant:

In l'Estoile's journal detailing the reign of Henri IV, we find the following passage:

« On montre depuis quelques jours, dans une maison près de Saint-Eustache, un homme nommé François Trouillu, âgé de trente cinq ans, qui aune corne sur la tête qui se recourbe en dedans, et rentrerait dans le crâne si de temps en temps on ne la coupait. Il dit qu’en naissant il n’avait pas cette corne, et qu’elle n’a commencé à paraître qu’à l’âge de sept à huit ans, et que la honte de cette difformité l’avait obligé de quitter son village, et de se cacher dans les forêts du Maine, où il travaillait aux charbonnières pour y gagner sa vie.

"We have been watching for a few days, in a house near Saint-Eustache, a man named François Trouillu, thirty-five years old, who has a horn on his head which curves inwards, that would start to pierce into his skull if from time to time it was not cut. He says when he was born he didn't have this horn, and that it only began to appear at the age of seven to eight years old, and that the shame of this deformity had forced him to leave his village, and hide in the forests of Maine, where he worked in the coal mines to earn a living.”

Jean de Beaumanoir, marquis de Laverdin, chassant un jour dans ses forêts, passa auprès de ces charbonnières. Les paysans qui travaillaient au charbon prirent la fuite au bruit des chasseurs. Le marquis de Laverdin, croyant que c’étaient des voleurs, les fit poursuivre ; on les arrête et on les conduit devant le marquis. Un de ses valets, ayant remarqué qu’un de ces pauvres paysans n’avait pas ôté son bonnet de sa tête, s’approche de lui, prend son bonnet, et le jette par terre en le menaçant ; mais ayant aperçu cette corne sur la tête, le marquis le fit conduire dans son château, et quelques jours après l’envoya au Roi, qui, après l’avoir fait voir à toute la Cour, l’a donné à un de ses valets d’écurie pour gagner de l’argent en le montrant au peuple.

Jean of Beaumanoir, the Marquis de Laverdin, out hunting one day in these forests, passed near the coal mines. The peasants who worked in the mines fled at the sound of the hunters. The Marquis de Laverdin, believing that they were thieves, had them pursued; once captured, they were brought before the Marquis. One of his servants, having noticed that one of these poor peasants did not remove his cap from his head as directed, came up to him and took his cap, which he then threateningly hurled at the ground; but having seen this horn on the man's head, the Marquis had him taken to his castle, and a few days later sent him to the King, who, after showing the horn to the whole Court, entrusted him to a stable boy, so that he might earn money by exhibiting the horned man to various townsfolk across the nation.”

Cet homme a le devant de la tête chauve, la barbe rousse et par flocons comme aussi les cheveux du derrière de la tête, et ressemble parfaitement à un satyre » (fig. 92).

The man was bald atop his head, had a small red beard which was as sparse as the tufts of hair on the back of his head, and looked perfectly like a satyr ”(fig. 92).

Ce pauvre homme fut si honteux d’être ainsi montré comme une bête curieuse qu’il en conçut un profond chagrin qui le conduisit au tombeau; cette triste fin n’excita pas même la pitié et l’on prolongea les risées qui l’avaient désespéré pendant sa vie, en gravant sur sa tombe dans le cimetière de Saint-Côme, cette plate épitaphe:

This poor man was so ashamed to be on display as a curious beast that he developed a deep sadness which led him to the tomb; such a sorry end did not even arouse pity, as the laughter that had tormented him for his entire life was prolonged by those who carved on his grave, in the cemetery of Saint-Côme, this flat epitaph:

Dans ce petit endroit à part
Gît un très-singulier cornard,
Car il le fut sans avoir femme.
Passant, priez Dieu pour son âme

 In this small plot apart,
Lies a strange horny fool*
who never had a wife.
Passer-by, pray to God for his soul.


*The original French epitaph uses the derogatory word cornard to describe the poor satyr-man. I have chosen to translate this line as “strange horny fool” in order to capture the word-play of the original French. Cornard can mean “poor sap” as well as “cuckhold,” but the word itself is very similar to cornes, the word for horn. It is likely that this shamelessly witty insult meant to connote not only the man’s pitiful life and sorry end, but also the fact of his being horned.
