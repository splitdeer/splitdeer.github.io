Arthur Éloffe appears in the records of history as a rather curious character.

Aside from "Histoire naturelle des cornes" (Natural History of Horns), he is also credited as the author of “Les Champignons Comestibles et Vénéneux” (A Guide to Edible and Poisonous Mushrooms, 1880).

Éloffe was born in 1826 in France, where he worked as a trained taxidermist in addition to running a naturalist preparatory business and a tiny school in the Rue de l’Ecole de Médecine 20, Paris. Éloffe taught his students how to dissect, clean, and preserve specimens. He published several works on a variety of topics- such as plants, animals and mushrooms- under the pseudonym Kroenishfranck.


[Dictionnaire de Biographie Francaise 12, 1519 pp.]


He gave himself the title of “professor of taxidermy,” though he neglected to mention any affiliation with an institution. The building of the “school” he ran also happened to be his apartment at 20 Rue de l'Ecole-de-Médicine. The building was adjacent to no. 10, the home & shop of another accomplished naturalist & entomologist of the early 19th century, Nérée Boubée. (Who, in 1845, established a publishing house that is still successful today, Maison d'édition Boubée.)

Boubée’s apartment, no. 10, is said to be the same apartment where Jean Paul Marat was assassinated by Charlotte Corday while in the bath. She hid a 5-inch long kitchen knife in her corset, which she then used to puncture his carotid artery.

Somehow, both the knife and the bathtub wound up at the Musée Grévin, a fascinatingly macabre wax museum at 10 Boulevard Montmartre in Paris, where they still sit today; arranged in a scene replete with life-sized wax renderings of both Marat and Corday.


(The murder scene as rendered in 2001, image courtesy of Atlas Obscura)


Many of Boubée’s specimens are still circulating today, passed along by different collectors. One assumes that many of the insects, birds, reptiles, fossils, and shells he collected and mounted in display boxes must be stored in the archives of museums and the various scientific institutions of Paris. That being said, I’ve found quite a few on Ebay auctions.

 
This particular display, labeled “Vintage Butterfly Display - Boubée Cie, Paris - various species - 39×25.5×5.5 cm” by the seller, sold to an anonymous bidder for €25.


Looks like someone decided it was time to clean out the closet of natural history…


Another auction lot titled “Antique microscope slides with microscope - Boubée, Paris - animal and botanical specimens - Cantharellus / Boletus / Morchela” was just sold for €185 in 2020.
